About the Quilter: Debbie Nichols Poulos

 The initial inspiration for Debbie’s quilts comes from the wide pallet of colors and patterns she finds in her extensive fabric collection. She collected fabrics for 11 years, some with a quilt in mind, but some for which she had no plan--buying a little of this, more of that, and a lot of something else. Before long her stash had grown well beyond her plans for specific quilts. Even though she’d occasionally purge her stack of leftovers she didn’t think she’d ever use, she still had piles of fabrics with no intended project. (In the first 11 years of quilting she made over 100 quilts, from lap to king size, for family and friends. Of her 13 grandchildren some now have as many as 5 or 6 quilts each. It comforts Debbie to know that when she is gone her loved ones will have quilts to remember her by.)

 Her quilting began in 1999 when a fellow Patwin Elementary School teacher started the group. During her first years of quilting she followed traditional designs to make quilts with hundreds of pieces. (Debbie has always had an affinity for fabric. The summer after 6th grade she took a Singer sewing class and for the next several years made most of her own clothing.)

 In the last few years, as she lost her ability to cut and sew, she started to make very simple half square triangle quilts, trying to get rid of her stash-- and a new “art form” was born. She believes these maze-like quilts are unique to her. Since moving to University Retirement Community (URC), Davis in 2011 she has not bought any fabric. Since then she has made 10-20 quilts a year and her stash is “still ample.”

 From this stash Debbie selects the “focus fabric” (a piece of fabric that will serve as a visual keystone for the entire quilt). She then selects pairings of light and dark fabrics that are harmonious with the focus fabric. In order to get enough fabric to make a quilt the desired size she sometimes finds one or two fabrics that are “a little off,” or are unexpected. She likes to challenge the eyes of her viewers. See if you can find these fabrics.

 For the last couple of years her friend JoAnn Diel has cut the fabrics and, after several steps, constructs 9.5” squares of 2 right-angled triangles, each composed of 1 of the fabric pairings. The process of combining different fabric pairings into squares will produce 4 to 9 unique sets of squares.

 The final step in the design process, where the magic happens, Debbie calls “the quilt dance” because it requires someone to move around placing blocks here and there on the carpet following her directions. The first step in the quilt dance was to create a “hook” in the center of the design utilizing the focus fabric.

 Once the hook has been established, Debbie selects from the sets of dark and light half square triangles to create a maze of contrasting colors and patterns. After the dance is complete, the squares are carefully collected in stacks. She sends them off to be assembled and quilted by Pat Fryer of Villa Rosa Designs. Find her website at this link.

 Today, Debbie relies on friends to assist with the tasks she can no longer perform. (She was diagnosed with ALS in 2006.) However, she directs every aspect of the creation of the work, has an amazing eye for color. and is a keen designer.

 The quilts in Amazing Mazes will indeed delight and surprise; but they will equally move you once you know the real amazing maze and life journey that led to their creation.
